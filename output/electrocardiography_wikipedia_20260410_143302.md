## Summary

Electrocardiography (ECG) is a diagnostic procedure that records the heart's electrical activity using specialized electrodes. This recording plots voltage against time, displaying characteristic waves—such as the P, QRS, and T waves—which represent the heart's electrical cycle of depolarization and repolarization. The resulting tracing provides crucial information about the heart's rhythm, rate, and electrical conduction system. Clinicians rely on ECG results to diagnose various cardiac issues, including arrhythmias, signs of myocardial damage, and electrolyte imbalances.

## Key Facts

* Electrocardiography (ECG) is a process using an electrocardiograph to produce a line graph showing the heart's electrical activity over repeated cardiac cycles.
* An ECG is an electrogram representing voltage versus time of the heart's electrical activity, detected using electrodes placed on the skin.
* ECG changes can indicate cardiac issues, including rhythm disturbances (e.g., atrial fibrillation), inadequate coronary artery blood flow (e.g., myocardial infarction), and electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hypokalemia).
* A conventional 12-lead ECG uses ten electrodes placed on the limbs and chest to measure the electrical potential from twelve different angles over time.
* The three main components of an ECG are the P wave (atrial depolarization), the QRS complex (ventricular depolarization), and the T wave (ventricular repolarization).
* An ECG can measure the heart rate and rhythm, assess the size/position of the heart chambers, and detect damage to the heart's muscle or conduction system.
* Indications for performing an ECG include chest pain, symptoms like shortness of breath or fainting, medication monitoring, and perioperative monitoring.
* Continuous ECG monitoring is used for critically ill patients or those with infrequently occurring cardiac arrhythmias.
* Mechanical cardiographs, developed in the 19th century, recorded heart movements but were limited by capturing all body movements, introducing errors.
* For asymptomatic adults, current evidence does not support routine ECG use for the prevention of cardiovascular disease.
